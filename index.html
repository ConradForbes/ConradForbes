03 The Lab

Growing meat
from scratch.

Before data, there was biology. My first significant research work was in cell-based agriculture - developing cellulose scaffolds from brewing industry by-products as substrates for cultured meat production.

The work lived at the intersection of material science, biology, and process engineering. We were building structures at the microscale to support living tissue. Precision mattered at every order of magnitude.

It taught me that the most interesting problems are rarely owned by a single discipline - and that the people who move between them tend to see things others miss.

04 The Shift

From molecules
to systems.

The move from biotech into data engineering wasn't a departure. It was a translation. The same rigour that makes good lab science makes good data infrastructure: reproducibility, traceability, understanding exactly what you're measuring and why.

Data became the medium. The underlying way of thinking - systems, constraints, cause and effect - stayed the same.

Cellulose Scaffolds for Cultured Meat

MSc Research — UCL · 2021–22

Developed biodegradable cellulose scaffolds from brewing industry by-products as growth substrates for cultivated muscle tissue. Work involved characterising scaffold porosity, mechanical properties, and cell adhesion behaviour across multiple iterations.

A low-cost, circular-economy scaffold substrate addressing a key manufacturing cost barrier in cultivated meat production.

I'm currently training for Ironman. Same logic applies.

How I build

  • Correctness before speed. A fast pipeline that produces wrong numbers is worse than no pipeline at all.
  • Understand the system, not just the task. Most engineering problems are downstream effects of upstream decisions.
  • Design for the next person. Code that only its author can maintain is a liability, not an asset.
  • Simplicity is the work. Complexity is easy. Clarity takes sustained effort.
