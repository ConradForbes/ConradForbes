Developing Computational Models for Brain Tissue Interactions

During my master's degree, I lead a project that developed computational models to compare the brain tissue interactions of a healthy person to a person who has suffered a traumatic brain injury.

A model of key areas of the brain vasculature was created using CAD software. The model was based on the posterior Circle of Willis and included the blood vessel wall and 2mm of surrounding tissue. The surrounding tissue was incorporated  to gain a better understanding of how the brain tissue reacted in different conditions.

Preliminary simulations were computed using CFD software to determine the blood flow rate and pressure throughout the model; the data obtained here was used to build thorough computational models of smaller model sections where FEA software and CFD software were used simultaneously to determine the blood flow characteristics and resulting vasculature deformation in both subjects.

Exoskeleton Design Project

During the final year of my undergraduate degree, I worked on a project to design an exoskeleton for a patient with lower-limb paralysis.

Before the project's design stage could begin, research on previously designed exoskeletons was conducted to determine the appropriate features that would be required on the exoskeleton to ensure that it was functional for its purpose.

CAD software was used to create the exoskeleton model, while FEA software was used to analyse the properties of the model. The waist segment of the model was made up of a rounded bar that wrapped around the back of the patient and housed the model's battery. The hip joints had two degrees of freedom, while the knee and ankle joints only had one. The foot was designed to be large enough to accommodate an average shoe, and it also had sensors to detect obstacles in front of the model.

Investigating OpenSim as a Gait Analysis Software

During my bachelor's degree dissertation project, I investigated whether a new software called OpenSim was suitable for gait analysis. OpenSim was a novel open-source software that claimed to be capable of calculating individual muscle forces in the human body during gait, a feat that no other all-in-one gait analysis software on the market had accomplished.

There was very little information available online about the OpenSim software, so extensive research was required to develop an understanding of the software before any conclusions could be drawn. The software received positional data as an input and projected it onto one of the software's models. The models were made up of the entire human skeleton as well as several muscles.

To convert the input data into the correct format for OpenSim, MATLAB was used. Once I had the OpenSim software working with my input data, I compared the resulting muscle force graphs to those found in the literature to determine the software's accuracy and precision. The data was very similar, so it was determined that the OpenSim software could be used as a gait analysis tool that also measures individual muscle forces. This would reduce the cost required to conduct high-quality gait analysis because you would no longer need to purchase additional equipment to measure muscle forces.

Biomedical Signal Processing

During my bachelor's degree final year, I worked on a project in which I used biomedical signal processing to determine the characteristics of several disorders.

MATLAB was used for the majority of the analysis for this project. To process data from various sources, several different signal processing methods and techniques were used. Patients with Alzheimer's disease and epilepsy were studied using electroencephalogram (EEG) signals. Patients with cardiac Autonomic Neuropathy were studied using electrocardiogram (ECG) signals. Hydrocephalus patients were studied using intracranial pressure (ICP) signals.

This project's aim was to develop high-quality analyses of biomedical signals in order to draw accurate conclusions about the characteristics of each disorder. The signals of the disordered subjects were compared to the signals of a healthy subject in all cases. Filtering, classical and modern spectral analysis, time-frequency analysis, discrete wavelet analysis, signal power spectrum estimation, and non-linear techniques were among the signal processing techniques employed.

Prosthetic Hand Design Project

During the second year of my undergraduate studies, I worked on a project in which I designed and manufactured a prosthetic hand for a young female amputee.

Significant research was required to ensure that the model was functional, made of suitable materials, and had the desired  dimensions. Two small servo motors located within the palm would  power the hand, and pressure sensors would be placed on the tips of the fingers.

The prosthetic model was designed and developed using CAD software, and its mechanical properties were determined using FEA analysis software. The finished model was a hand with five string-actuated fingers. The thumb and index finger were controlled by one servo motor, while the remaining three fingers were controlled by the other. The design included pressure sensors and a temperature sensor to ensure that the prosthetic 'shut-off' if the model was subjected to excessive temperatures or force.
